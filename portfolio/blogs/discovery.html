Discovery Phase

Background

Brainstormed Ideas

We were tasked to find a problem that we face as University of Auckland students in a group of four. To do this, we initially created a large brainstorm coming up with a wide range of ideas individually, then brought our ideas together and found that we each had some common trends running through the problems we faced.
We had all faced transportation issue when commuting into the city campus. We also all faced navigation issues while trying to find our next class around campus. And we had all faced a plethora of enrolment issues.
Since we all felt strongly about the three topics above, we narrowed the target problem down to those three, then even further filtered them to the enrolment issues; this will be the problem we decided to focus on.


The Problem

Defining the problem

Once we found our topic, we struggled to define our problem well since there are so many issues students face when enrolling, but we found that it broke down into a few aspects.

  1. Access to information
  2. Ease of navigation
  3. Clear degree structuring

So we decided to create a better enrolment experience for students.
For our observation, given the nature of the problem space, we couldn't easily observe people naturally encountering the problem since most people enrol at home and will enrol closer to the semester starting. So we had casual conversations with past and present university students to understand their feelings about the enrolment process; we also reflected as a group on our own experience while enrolling.


Initial problem analysis

Interview notes

Since we had all faced this issue numerous times, we could easily pick up on many surface-level problems quickly from a casual conversation and find aspects to the design. We provided information which we wished was improved. Amongst our group, we were able to do some peer interviews to get information about what we each liked and disliked and come up with an initial batch of ideas as to what was missing from the current design.

We then concluded our class time by assigning tasks for us each to do by the next meeting. Two of us would perform empathy (3 interviews each) conversations, while the other two would do secondary research and a survey. Although the assignment called for less than this, we felt that performing each of these tasks would be valuable for different aspects and would be useful to get a broader understanding of the problem.

Although it was recommended that we have two people at each interview (one to write notes and one to ask the questions) due to unforeseen circumstances, we could not do this, so we did individual interviews instead.

After conducting team interviews to understand what issues the team members face while enrolling and conducting three brief, casual interviews with current design students, I Performed three in-depth semi-formal interviews. Two interviewees were current students, and one was a past student, two studied computer science and one studied engineering.



Observations and Insights


The observations I found from my interviews were:

  1. Students struggle to understand which courses they need to complete.
  2. Students don't know what the course content will be.
  3. There is no access to which kinds of jobs a course is aimed towards.
  4. Students worry they will need extra semesters if they pick the incorrect courses.
  5. It's hard to apply for concessions since the course coordinator isn't listed on the course calendar.
  6. Students have to cross-reference between many sources of information.
  7. It takes a long time to enrol and pick courses for each semester.
  8. There's inconsistent information for past students about courses
  9. Students get lots of information from 3rd party sources
  10. Students feel frustrated when attempting to enrol into courses

And my insights were:

  1. Students want an easy, structured layout that presents basic info unless the user wants more in-depth information.
  2. Picking courses can feel overwhelming.
  3. Most students are happy following the most common degree path and most straightforward route if it means they know they've chosen the right courses.
  4. If there were a 1st party way to get information about a course, students would use it.
  5. There is a large gap between what students need and what is currently being provided.
