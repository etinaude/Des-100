Chess

Gears

The final piece

Initially, I started by spending many hours attempting to perform topology optimisation and generative design in Fusion 360 to fit a different movie’s aesthetic. Unfortunately, the geometries of a chess piece do not lend themselves towards generative design. I restarted the project and picked the sequel to the Phantom of the Opera, “Love Never Dies”. This focuses on three main characters who are caught in a “love triangle” and thus have a dynamic that doesn’t work. If any one of the three were to be removed, then the dynamic would be near-frictionless. I have represented this using the groupings of three gears around the piece. Additionally, there is a theme running throughout the film of strange mechanisms in a near steampunk fashion. This was another reason why I decided to use gears.


Mask

Printed model

Instead of being crested by a cross like a regular king, this ha a fattened “phantom mask”. This is both because the mask is a major point of tension throughout the movie and because Erik (the phantom) rules “phantasma” (the location the film is set) and acts as its king. Additionally, he controls everything throughout the movie and ends up “on top,” which is another reason why the mask is the centrepiece. The mask has also become a symbol of musical theatre which is an essential aspect of the film as it’s performed as a musical theatre performance but produced as a movie.


Final design

I ended up going through 35 versions of the piece to eventually landed on this design which I felt truly encapsulated the film’s aesthetic and was not ambiguous as to which piece as being displayed. The piece’s base and overall form were inspired by a Staunton chess set (one of the most common sets); this was done to ensure the player knows which piece they were playing, as the design needs to be functional and easy to use.
