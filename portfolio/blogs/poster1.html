The Haiku

This is a poster using primitive geometric designs to abstract the haiku by an anonymous poet:

haikus are really dumb
seriously, the worst poems
they have no meaning

- Anonymous ("Haikus", n.d)

This haiku has a few themes running through it; the first is irony. The poet could have chosen any type of poetry, but they decided on a haiku. Second, there is an element of irritation as they are insulting an art form. Third, there is an air of acceptance as the word "poems" indicates that the poet does acknowledge that while they do not appreciate haikus, they understand that others do. This acceptance is also shown by the fact that this is written in the form of a haiku.


Design Considerations

a range of prototypes

I used a digital tool to prototype and develop ideas that could be used in the final poster. Through this, I repetitively iterated on my designs until I found a design that I felt captured the haiku's feel and settled on this design. I used the white circle to emphasise the inside triangles over the outside ones and create a balance between the outside and inside triangles since they are the same size and there are the same amount. After some feedback, I created another design iteration as it was found that the white circle was overpowering, so I made the whole design smaller so there would be a larger black area surrounding it.

Shapes


different shapes used in the design

As the design focuses on geometric primitives, shapes are obviously fairly important. The design consists of 3 large triangles implied by the groupings of 2 small white triangles and one small black triangle; I've shown this in red. Then there is one central triangle outlined by three small black triangles (highlighted in green), and lastly, there are the negative space triangles shown in purple. This totals to 7 large triangles and 12 small triangles. The triangles form a repetition and circular pattern. The repetition is furthered by the use of only two shapes. The cutout style is similar to that of figure 18e by one of Wucius Wong's students (Wong, 2008)

Kanizsa triangle (Kanizsa, 1955)

The red triangles use the gestalt principle of closure (Todorovic, 2008) to create the circles indicated in green. The gestalt principle of continuation for the triangles similar to the "Kanizsa triangle" (Kanizsa, 1955), which is a famous design often used to illustrate this principle. Although in my design, it's mainly the circles being closed by the triangles, These circles also break the large triangles into smaller ones and aid in the repetition. The main white circle is used to put the centre at the top of the visual hierarchy, and lastly, the central circle shown in yellow. This central one forms a "radioactive" symbol with the triangles.
